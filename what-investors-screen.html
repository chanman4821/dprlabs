Funding

What investors actually screen AI startups on (2026).

Investors are no longer impressed by “we use AI.” They want to know what gets stronger as you grow: data, evals, distribution, workflow ownership, margins, and team judgment.

This is written founder-to-founder. It is not a promise that any investor will fund you. It is a checklist for the questions that tend to separate an AI feature from an AI company. Use it before a fundraise, before a board meeting, or before you spend three months building the wrong moat.

1. Defensibility: what compounds?

The first screen is simple: why does this get harder to copy with every customer? “We call a model API” is not enough. “Every customer workflow creates labeled exceptions that improve our eval set, routing logic, and domain-specific recommendations” is closer.

Defensibility can come from proprietary workflow data, distribution, deep domain integration, compliance know-how, switching costs, network effects, or a team with rare taste in a hard market. It usually does not come from a prompt alone. Prompts matter, but they are rarely the moat by themselves.

Self-diagnosis: if a well-funded competitor could rebuild your demo in a week from your landing page, what would they still not have? If the answer is “nothing,” your story needs more work.

Board-ready sentence: “Our moat is not the model. It is the proprietary review data, evaluation harness, and workflow distribution created every time a customer uses the product.” Only say this if it is true.

2. Owned data and evals

Investors increasingly ask about evals because evals reveal whether the company can tell good output from bad output. A pretty demo can hide weak judgment. An eval set shows taste, precision, and learning speed.

Good answers sound concrete: “We have 600 reviewed examples across the top five customer workflows, with labels for accuracy, policy fit, tone, and escalation risk. We run them before each release and track cost, latency, and failure reasons.” A weaker answer is: “The team checks outputs manually and they look good.”

Data ownership also matters. If your most valuable examples, labels, corrections, and usage traces live inside a vendor account you cannot fully export, you may not control the thing that makes the business better. That is not always fatal early. It is a gap you should name honestly and fix deliberately.

In 2026, “we have data” is vague. “We own the feedback loop that turns usage into better decisions” is the real claim.

3. Inference unit economics

AI margins can look wonderful in a slide deck and ugly in production. Investors will ask what it costs to serve one customer, one task, one document, one claim, one support answer, or one generated report. You should know the unit, the average cost, the heavy-tail cost, and what happens when usage grows.

A practical model has at least four rows: model calls, retrieval or search, human review, and infrastructure. Human review belongs in the model because many AI products quietly depend on people fixing the hard cases. That can be fine if priced correctly. It is dangerous if hidden.

Hypothetical example: if a workflow saves an analyst 18 minutes but costs $2.40 in model calls and 6 minutes of review, the business case may still be strong. But the board conversation is different than if the same workflow costs 8 cents and needs review only on exceptions.

Know these numbers: cost per completed task, p95 latency, review rate, failure rate, gross margin at current usage, and the usage level where your current architecture breaks.

4. Distribution beats cleverness

Many AI startups are technically impressive and commercially fragile. Investors look for a wedge: a specific buyer, a painful workflow, a reachable channel, and a reason the customer will trust you with the work. “Everyone needs productivity” is not a wedge. “Regional insurance brokers spend three hours per carrier submission and already send us the documents” is a wedge.

Distribution can be founder-led sales, an existing community, integrations, channel partners, data partnerships, or a product-led loop. The important thing is that it is specific. If your first 20 customers are clear, your product decisions get sharper. If your buyer is abstract, the roadmap gets noisy.

Self-diagnosis: can you name the buyer, their current workaround, the trigger event that makes them search, and the budget owner? If not, your AI feature may be looking for a market instead of solving one.

01

Team judgment

Investors screen whether the team can make product, data, and risk tradeoffs. AI products fail at the edges: bad inputs, stale data, policy conflicts, hallucinated confidence, and ambiguous customer intent. Teams that can name those edges earn trust.

02

Honest positioning

Do not claim research depth if you are a workflow company. Do not claim full automation if humans review critical outputs. The best founders are precise about what the system does today and what gets stronger next.

03

Fundraising gap list

Before you raise, write your five weakest answers: moat, data rights, evals, margins, distribution, or team gaps. Fix what you can. Name the rest plainly and show the plan.

Founder self-check

  • Can we explain why usage makes the product better?
  • Do we own the examples and labels that prove quality?
  • Can we show a repeatable eval, not just a demo?
  • Do we know cost per completed customer task?
  • Is our buyer specific enough to find this week?
  • Can we name what a competitor cannot copy quickly?

If those answers are weak, it does not mean the company is weak. It means the next build should create evidence, not just features.
