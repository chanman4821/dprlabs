Playbook

How to pick the ONE AI feature worth building first.

The best first AI feature is not the flashiest idea. It is the smallest workflow that happens often, can be measured, has bounded data, and makes a buyer or board member say, “I understand why this matters.”

Most AI roadmaps fail because the first project tries to prove everything at once: product vision, data strategy, automation, personalization, and a future platform. That is too much weight for a pilot. A useful pilot should answer one question with evidence: can this workflow be improved enough to justify the next investment?

The five filters

Use these filters before choosing a feature. Score each idea from 1 to 5. Anything below 18 out of 25 is probably not your first pilot, even if it is interesting.

High frequency. The workflow should happen many times per week. AI improves through examples, review, and repetition. A task that happens twice a quarter may matter, but it will not give you fast feedback.

Measurable outcome. Pick something with a visible before-and-after number: minutes saved, tickets resolved, quote accuracy, review time, conversion lift, error rate, or cost per completed task. If you cannot measure it, you cannot defend it in a board meeting.

Demoable in one minute. The result should be easy to show. A founder should be able to pull up one real-looking, sanitized example and explain the old way, the new way, and the number moved. A good demo is not theater; it is compressed evidence.

Bounded data. The data should live in a known set of places: a CRM object, a folder of PDFs, a support queue, a database table, a few spreadsheets. “All company knowledge” is not bounded. “The last 500 support tickets and the refund policy” is bounded.

Clear owner. Someone on the business side must care enough to review outputs, answer edge cases, and adopt the result. No owner means no feedback loop. No feedback loop means no improvement.

Simple scoring rule: first pilot candidates should be frequent, measurable, demoable, bounded, and owned. If one of those is missing, fix the scope before you build.

Good vs bad first pilots

Bad first pilot: “Build an AI chief of staff for the whole company.” It sounds strategic, but the scope is foggy. The data is scattered. The owner is everyone and no one. Success becomes subjective: did it feel useful?

Better first pilot: “Draft the weekly renewal-risk summary for customer success managers from CRM notes, support tickets, product usage, and billing status.” This is narrower. It happens often. The data sources are known. A customer success leader can review quality. The metric could be prep time per account or number of at-risk accounts reviewed before the weekly meeting.

Bad first pilot: “Automate all investor research.” Too broad, too many edge cases, and too easy to generate confident nonsense.

Better first pilot: “For each target investor, produce a one-page fit brief using only approved sources: fund stage, check size, sector focus, recent deals, and warm-intro paths.” The output is constrained, reviewable, and immediately useful for fundraising prep.

Bad first pilot: “Replace our underwriting team.” That creates fear, legal risk, and a giant acceptance problem.

Better first pilot: “Pre-check applications for missing documents, policy mismatches, and manual-review flags before an underwriter opens the file.” The human still decides. The system removes drag and creates a clean audit trail.

Selection framework

Pick the first feature in four passes.

01

List painful workflows, not ideas

Write down recurring work people complain about: copying data between tools, reading long documents, triaging inbound requests, preparing reports, checking compliance, summarizing customer history.

02

Attach a number to each one

For every workflow, name the current cost: hours per week, wait time, error rate, missed revenue, review backlog, or number of handoffs. Drop anything with no measurable baseline.

03

Find the smallest useful slice

Do not automate the department. Automate one decision-support step, one drafting step, one audit step, or one routing step. The slice should fit inside two weeks and still matter if it works.

04

Choose the owner before the build

The owner is the person who will say what good looks like, review misses, and drive adoption. If that person cannot make time during the pilot, pick a different workflow.

Copyable scoping worksheet

Fill this in before your first build conversation:
Workflow: What recurring task are we improving?
Owner: Who reviews quality and owns adoption?
Users: Who touches the workflow weekly?
Baseline: What number describes the current pain?
Target: What change would make the pilot worth it?
Data: Which exact systems or files are in scope?
Exclusions: Which sources, decisions, and edge cases are out of scope?
Human review: Where does a person approve, edit, or reject?
Demo: What one-minute example would prove the value?
Handover: Who runs it after the pilot?

One practical rule: if you cannot complete that worksheet in plain English, the scope is not ready. That does not mean the idea is bad. It means the first version is too foggy to build responsibly.

The first pilot is not meant to become the whole product. It is meant to create one honest proof point your team can build on.
