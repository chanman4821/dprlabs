The problem

AI coding tools race to a demo, then break

Today's AI coding tools are quick to a demo, then quietly ship the four things that break real software. Nothing checks the work, nothing remembers what happened, and there's no record of what changed.

SLOPCompiles, but doesn’t do what was asked
HALLUCINATIONMade-up imports, functions, paths
STALLLoops and burns tokens, never finishes
DRIFTA 5-line ask becomes a 500-line PR

What NR3 does

Tell it what you want. It does the work.

You describe what you want. NR3 breaks it into tasks, gives each one to the worker that's best at it, builds it, checks it, and fixes what fails, writing down every step.

Every check is there to catch one of those four problems. Instead of reading through every code change, you get a short record of what it did and whether it passed.

How it works

One brain, many workers, one shared memory

plan→assign →build→check →fix↺not good enough? back it goes

One AI brain plans the work, then hands each task to the worker best suited to it. Anything that isn't good enough gets sent back, not shipped.

It remembers what worked on past jobs and uses it on the next one. Built-in stops keep it safe: a kill switch, a spending cap, a limit on how much runs at once, and tight control over the processes it starts.

Why it's hard to copy

It gets better every time it works

Fast tools that skip the checking just create rework. NR3 does the opposite: it compares every result to what actually happened and remembers it, so it gets better with each job instead of drifting. That mix, checking the work and learning from it, is much harder to copy than a quick demo. And because NR3 runs on top of whichever AI models are best, it improves every time the models do, instead of competing with them.

Proof

Already used on real work

DPR, an AI consulting firm, is a design partner: it runs NR3 on real, paid client work (not lab demos) and helps shape the product. One real run, with client details removed: 305 files changed, 60 tasks done, about 18 hours of compute. NR3 has even fixed its own bugs while running.

Market & money

Who buys it, and how it charges

Any team that builds or maintains software can use it. The plan: a base platform fee plus a charge for each build that passes the checks, with seats for teams. DPR is the first design partner using it on real work and can vouch for it.

Honest status — what's real vs. what we're building

The building part works today; the self-checking loop is the raise

Real now: it plans, builds, and checks real work, with the safety stops and the shared memory recording every run. Still building: the full loop where it learns from every result on its own and repairs itself without a person watching. A senior human still approves the calls that matter, and NR3 doesn't claim to run itself yet. Being upfront about that is the point: finishing that loop is exactly what we're raising money to do.
