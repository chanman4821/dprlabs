## Section 4 — How we measure

- **Eyebrow:** How we measure
- **Headline:** A number you can't re-check is marketing, not proof.
- **Body:** We set the baseline from your real data before we touch anything. At the end we
  run the system on a real, human-checked sample and report the same number the same way. We
  write the method down — the sample size, who checked it, and every caveat — so you, or
  anyone, can reproduce it. Honesty about a small sample reads as more credible than a big
  round number, so we show the sample size, not hide it.
- **What you can verify today:** the proof here is the method — and it's all on this page. The
  scorecard shape, the blank template you can fill in from your own data, and the plain-English
  measurement walkthrough above are the whole argument; nothing is hidden behind a case study
  you can't read. You can also check a real, sanitized run of our delivery engine on Built on
  Jarvis. When our founders publish their own profiles — GitHub, talks, shipped work — they link
  here too, real and checkable or not at all.
- **Verifiable now (real links, live today):** The blank scorecard template → Section 3 of this
  page · A real run → `jarvis.html` · How a pilot works → `method.html`

## Section 5 — What could go wrong

- **Eyebrow:** What could go wrong
- **Headline:** The honest limits, said out loud.
- **Body (as a short list):**
  - Your workflow may not have a clean number to move. We'll tell you before you pay.
  - Some tasks need judgment a model can't yet be trusted with. We say no to those.
  - A two-week pilot proves direction, not a five-year SLA. Scale is a separate conversation.
  - One anonymized pilot is one data point. We won't dress it up as a track record.
- **Why this is here:** naming the failure modes disarms skepticism better than praise does.
  A vendor who tells you what could go wrong is easier to trust than one who won't.
