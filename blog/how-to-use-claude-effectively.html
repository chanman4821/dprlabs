How to use Claude effectively: a plain-English guide

Claude, made by Anthropic, is one of the best AI assistants for real work — especially writing, reading long documents, and thinking through a problem carefully. But like every tool of this kind, you get out what you put in. A lazy request gets a generic answer; a well-set-up request gets something genuinely useful. Here is how to set it up well, in plain English.

1. Tell it who it should be

The fastest way to change the answer is to give Claude a role. "You are a careful bookkeeper." "You are a friendly customer-support writer." "You are a blunt editor." One short sentence shifts the tone, the vocabulary, and what it pays attention to. Start most requests this way.

2. Give it the context it can't guess

Claude does not know your business, your customers, or last week's meeting. It only knows what you put in front of it. So paste the background: what you're trying to do, who it's for, and any facts that matter. The difference between a generic answer and a great one is almost always the amount of real context you gave it.

3. Ask for the exact format you want

If you don't say how you want the answer, you get Claude's default — usually a few tidy paragraphs. So ask: "Give me five bullet points." "Put it in a table." "Keep it under 100 words." "Write it as an email." Naming the shape you want saves you a round of edits every time.

4. Show it one good example

One example of what "good" looks like beats a paragraph of instructions. Paste an email you liked and say "match this tone." Show a product description you're happy with and say "write three more like this." Examples teach faster than adjectives.

5. Use its real strength: long documents

Claude is unusually good at reading long things. Paste a contract, a report, or a big email thread and ask for a summary, the key risks, or the three decisions buried inside it. This is one of the highest-value things a small team can do with it — turning a wall of text into the two minutes that actually matter.

One caution: don't paste anything confidential — customer data, contracts, secrets — into any AI tool until you've checked its privacy settings and terms. We cover that properly in the free Learn AI guide.

6. Work in passes, not one big shot

Don't expect the perfect answer on the first try, and don't write a giant instruction to force it. Get a rough draft, then steer: "shorter," "less formal," "add a section on pricing," "cut the last paragraph." Treat it like editing alongside a fast writer. Three quick passes beat one enormous prompt.

7. Make it check its own work

After it answers, ask: "What might be wrong or missing here?" or "What would a skeptic say about this?" Claude will often catch its own weak spots and soften anything it overstated. It's a simple habit that noticeably raises quality — but it is not a guarantee.

8. Save the prompts that work

When a request gives you a great result, keep it. Paste it into a notes doc with a title. Over a few weeks you'll build a small library of "known-good" prompts your whole team can reuse, so nobody starts from a blank page. Reusing beats reinventing.

What Claude can't do — be honest about the limits

Getting good at Claude also means knowing where to stop trusting it:

  • It can be confidently wrong. It can invent facts, names, numbers, and even quotes, and state them with total confidence. Always verify anything that has to be exactly right.
  • It doesn't know today's news unless it's connected to the web in that moment. Its built-in knowledge has a cut-off date.
  • It's not your lawyer, doctor, or accountant. It's great for a first draft or a plain-English explanation; the final judgment on anything serious stays with a qualified human.

Used this way — a clear role, real context, an example, the format you want, and a couple of passes — Claude stops feeling like a novelty and starts saving you real time every day. That's the whole game: not clever tricks, just giving a capable assistant enough to work with, and checking what matters.
