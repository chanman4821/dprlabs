Lesson 2 · Learn AI

Your first 15 minutes with an AI assistant

6 min readPlain English

By the end

You'll have gone from "I've never really used it" to getting a genuinely useful result — in about fifteen minutes.

Setting up AI is far simpler than people expect. There's nothing to install and nothing to configure. You sign up on a website, type in a box, and read what comes back. That's the whole thing. Here it is, start to finish.

  1. 2 min

    Pick one tool

    Any of these free versions is a fine place to start: Claude (claude.ai), ChatGPT (chatgpt.com), or Gemini (gemini.google.com). Just pick one — you can try the others later. (We go deep on Claude in a separate post.)

  2. 3 min

    Make an account

    Sign up with your email or a Google login. The free plan is more than enough for learning. No credit card needed to start.

  3. 1 min

    Find the box

    You'll see one big text box that says something like "Message" or "Ask anything." That's where you type. That is the entire interface — don't overthink it.

  4. 5 min

    Give it a real task

    Don't test it with trivia. Give it something from your actual day. For example: paste some messy notes and say, "Turn these rough notes into a short, polite email to a customer." Watch what it does.

  5. 4 min

    Talk back to it

    You're not stuck with the first answer. Reply like you would to a person: "Make it shorter." "Warmer tone." "Add a line about the refund." Each reply improves it. This back-and-forth is where the real value is.

What this looks like in real life

You type: "Here are my notes — customer upset, order #4471 arrived late, I want to apologise, offer 10% off their next order, keep it short."

It writes a clean apology email in seconds.

You reply: "A bit warmer, and add that we've shipped it today." It fixes it. Two minutes, done — and better than the blank page you were staring at.

The three ways people get stuck

  • Overthinking the setup. There's no install and no settings to master. Sign up and start typing.
  • Starting with a vague one-liner. "Write an email" gives generic mush. Give it the details — Lesson 3 shows the simple recipe.
  • Giving up after one reply. The first answer is a draft, not the final word. The good stuff comes from telling it what to change.

Takeaway

Setup is just three things: sign up, type a real task, and reply to improve it. Fifteen minutes is enough to get something you'd actually use.

Try this today: take one small task you did this week and redo it with AI. Reply at least twice to shape the answer before you judge it.
