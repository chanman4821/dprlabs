The one-number principle

If we can't name the number, the pilot isn't ready.

Most AI projects fail because nobody agreed what "working" means. So we don't start until we've written down one measurable outcome — reply time, minutes per invoice, hours saved per week — with the exact way we'll measure it. One number keeps everyone honest: it decides, in plain sight, whether the pilot worked. That number is agreed in writing before any build. If we can't name it, the pilot isn't ready, and we'll tell you plainly instead of taking the work.

What makes a good number: specific and countable. "Feels faster" is not a number. "Minutes per invoice, sampled over 400 invoices" is.

Open measurement & ownership

A number you can't re-check isn't proof.

We set the baseline from your real data before we touch anything, then report the same number the same way at the end. We write the method down — sample size, who checked it, and the caveats — so you, or anyone, can reproduce it. When we hand off, you own the code and the knowledge. You can fire us and keep everything: the system runs on your infra, your repo, your accounts, with no dependency on us to keep it alive.

"You own it" is architecture, not a promise. We build on your accounts from day one, so there's nothing to migrate at handover.

When we say no

We'd rather lose the work than sell you a pilot that can't win.

  • If a simple script would fix it, you don't need a pilot — and we'll tell you.
  • If the data isn't there yet, there's no clean number to move. We wait.
  • If no one on your side will own the result, the handover has nowhere to land.

Saying no up front is cheaper for both of us than a pilot that was never going to reach a number.
