Compliance & Governance

What is AI governance and compliance — and does your company need it yet?

ExplainerLast reviewed Jul 8, 20266 min read

In short

Governance is the set of simple controls that keep an AI system accountable — knowing what it does, catching when it's wrong, logging its decisions, and keeping a human in the loop where it matters. Compliance is being able to prove that to a regulator or auditor. Most small companies need a light version of both sooner than they expect — especially with customer data or decisions that affect people or money.

"AI governance" sounds like something only banks worry about. It isn't. Strip away the jargon and it's just the handful of habits that stop an AI system from quietly doing the wrong thing in your name. Here's what it means, and how to tell whether you need it now.

Governance

Your own internal controls: what the AI is allowed to do, how you catch mistakes, what gets logged, and who is responsible when something goes wrong.

Compliance

Meeting outside rules: being able to show a regulator, auditor, or customer that your controls exist and actually work.

The everyday risks, in plain terms

  • Data. What goes into the AI, and where its outputs end up — the thing you least want leaking is often the thing someone pastes in first.
  • Accuracy. AI can be confidently wrong. Without a check, a bad answer ships as if it were true.
  • Accountability. If the AI makes a decision that affects a customer, someone on your side has to be able to explain and stand behind it.

A minimum viable governance setup

You don't need a policy binder. A small company can cover the basics with five things:

  • Log what the AI decides, so there's a record you can review.
  • Put a human check on the high-stakes outputs — money, legal, customer-facing.
  • Write one page: what the system does and what can go wrong.
  • Control who can access it and what data it can touch.
  • Have a way to switch it off or roll it back if it misbehaves.

When AI is built into your systems properly, these aren't extra work bolted on at the end — the audit trail, the human-in-the-loop step, and the docs are part of the build from day one. Adding them later, after something has gone wrong, is always harder.

Do you need it now, or later?

Signs you need it now

  • You work in a regulated field — health, finance, legal, insurance
  • The AI touches customer or personal data
  • Its output affects a decision about a person or money
  • A customer or partner has started asking how it works

Probably fine to keep light for now

  • Internal drafting and brainstorming with no sensitive data
  • A human reviews everything before it leaves the building
  • Low stakes if an answer is occasionally wrong

The honest rule: the more your AI touches people, money, or private data, the sooner light governance stops being optional — and the cheaper it is to build in early.
