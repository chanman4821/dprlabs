Getting Started

AI for small business: a practical, honest starting guide

GuideLast reviewed Jul 8, 20267 min read

In short

For a small business, the win from AI isn't "transform everything" — it's picking one or two repetitive tasks, using AI to do them faster, checking the output, and keeping it simple. Start with off-the-shelf tools you can use today; only pay to build something custom when a task is core to your business and you do it over and over.

Most "AI for small business" advice is either breathless hype or a list of 50 tools you'll never open. Here's the grounded version. You don't need a strategy, a budget, or a data team. You need one annoying, repetitive task and fifteen minutes.

Start with the pain, not the tech

Don't ask "how do I use AI?" Ask "what do I do every week that's repetitive and eats time?" Answering emails, writing the same kinds of posts, summarizing documents, chasing invoices, first drafts of anything. Pick the one that annoys you most. That's where AI earns its keep — not in some grand transformation.

What small businesses actually use AI for well

Drafting & rewriting

Emails, product descriptions, social posts, job ads — turn a rough idea into a solid first draft in seconds.

Summarizing

Long emails, reports, contracts, meeting notes — pull out the key points and the decisions.

Customer replies

Draft answers to common questions in your voice, with a human check before they send.

Organizing information

Turn messy notes into a clean list, a table, or a plan you can actually act on.

Use off-the-shelf tools first

Before anyone sells you a custom build, use what already exists. A free or cheap plan of ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini — plus the AI features increasingly built into the tools you already pay for — covers most small-business needs. Learn to use those well first. Our free Learn AI guide walks you through it, step by step, in plain English.

The build-vs-use test

Only pay to build a custom AI system when a task is core to your business, repeated a lot, and measurable. If it's occasional or low-stakes, an off-the-shelf tool is the smarter, cheaper answer — and an honest partner will tell you so.

When it's worth building something custom

Off-the-shelf hits a ceiling when the task is high-volume, tied to your own data and systems, and central to how you make money. That's when a custom build — wired into your tools, owned by you — starts to pay for itself. Before you commit, it's worth knowing how to measure whether it actually worked.

Keep your data safe

One rule to protect you from the most common mistake: don't paste customer data, passwords, or anything confidential into a public AI tool until you've checked its privacy settings. If AI touches customer or personal data, it's also worth a quick look at basic AI governance — even a one-page version.

Measure whether it's actually helping

The honest test is the same one big companies should use: did a number you care about actually move? Time saved on a task, replies handled per day, hours back in your week. If you can't point to a real change, it's a toy, not a tool. Keep the ones that move a number; drop the rest.
