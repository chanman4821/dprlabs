Compliance & Governance

AI governance framework, explained

ExplainerLast reviewed Jul 8, 20266 min read

In short

An "AI governance framework" sounds heavy, but it's just a written, repeatable answer to four questions: what are we allowed to use AI for, how do we check it's right, what do we log, and who's accountable. You don't need a 50-page binder — you need those four answers written down and actually followed.

The word "framework" scares people into thinking they need a compliance department. They don't. A framework is simply the set of rules you'd want in place so that AI can't quietly do something dumb or risky in your company's name — written down once, so it's the same every time. Here's what actually goes in one.

The four questions every framework answers

  1. What are we allowed to use AI for?

    Which tasks and tools are approved, and which are off-limits. Naming the green and red zones up front stops the "someone pasted the whole client list into a chatbot" problem.

  2. How do we check it's right?

    Where a human reviews the output before it's used, and how you'd catch a confident mistake. High-stakes work — money, legal, customer-facing — always gets a check.

  3. What do we log?

    A record of what the AI did and decided, so you can review it later, explain it to a customer, or show an auditor. If it's not written down, it didn't happen.

  4. Who's accountable?

    A named person who owns each AI use — not "the AI decided," but "this is ours and here's who stands behind it."

A minimal framework you can write in an afternoon

Answer the four questions on a single page for each way you use AI: the approved tool, the data rule, the review step, and the owner. That one page is your framework. You can grow it later, but a short document that people actually follow beats a thick one that sits in a drawer.

Where the big frameworks fit

If you want an established model to build on, the two most referenced are the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (a voluntary US government framework) and ISO/IEC 42001 (an international standard for AI management systems). They're more detailed versions of the same four ideas — map your risks, measure them, manage them, and govern who's responsible. For most small companies, start with the one-pager above and reach for these when a customer, investor, or regulator asks for a recognised standard.

Build it in, don't bolt it on

The cheapest time to add governance is while the AI system is being built — the logging, the human-check step, and the access rules become part of the design instead of a scramble after something goes wrong. Retro-fitting controls onto a black box you already shipped is always harder and more expensive.
