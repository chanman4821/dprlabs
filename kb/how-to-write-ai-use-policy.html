Compliance & Governance

How to write a simple AI use policy for your team

How-toLast reviewed Jul 8, 20265 min read

In short

An AI use policy is a short document that tells your team what they can and can't do with AI tools — mostly what data is safe to paste in, what needs a human check, and who to ask. One page is enough to start, and it's better to have a short one people follow than a long one they ignore.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: your team is probably already using AI at work, with or without your say-so. A use policy isn't about slowing them down — it's about drawing a couple of bright lines so nobody accidentally pastes a customer's private data into a public chatbot. You can write a useful one in an afternoon.

What to include

  • Approved toolsWhich AI tools are okay to use for work, and which aren't. A short allow-list beats a vague "use good judgment."
  • What never to paste inThe clear red line: customer data, passwords, contracts, anything confidential — unless it's an approved tool with the right privacy settings.
  • What needs a human checkWhich outputs a person must review before they're used — anything customer-facing, legal, financial, or a decision about a person.
  • DisclosureWhen you'll tell customers or staff that AI was involved, if it matters for your work.
  • Who owns the outputA reminder that AI output is a draft your team is responsible for — not something to ship unread.
  • Who to askA named person for questions and exceptions, so people don't just guess.
  • How to ask for an exceptionA simple way to say "can I use X for Y?" so the policy bends without breaking.

A one-page starting template

AI use policy — [Company] — [date] 1. Approved tools: [list]
2. Never paste in: customer data, passwords, contracts, [add yours]
3. Always human-checked before use: [customer-facing / legal / financial / …]
4. Disclose AI use when: [cases, or "not required"]
5. You own what you send: AI output is a draft you're responsible for.
6. Questions & exceptions: ask [name / channel]
7. Review date: [every 6 months]

Keep it short and enforceable

A policy nobody reads protects no one. Fit it on a page, use plain words, and make the two rules that actually matter — what not to paste, and what needs a human check — impossible to miss. You can always add detail later if a real situation calls for it.

Review it as the tools change

AI tools change monthly. Put a review date on the policy and actually keep it — a new tool, a new feature, or a new type of work can each move a bright line. A living one-pager beats a perfect document that's already out of date.

Note: this is practical guidance, not legal advice. If you're in a regulated field or handling sensitive data, have a qualified professional review your policy before you rely on it.
