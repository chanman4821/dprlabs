Measuring AI

How to measure the ROI of an AI project (without fooling yourself)

How-toLast reviewed Jul 8, 20267 min read

In short

Real AI ROI is simple to state and easy to fake. Pick one number your business already cares about, measure it honestly before you build, measure it again after on fresh data a human checks, and write down the sample size and caveats. If you can't measure it that way, you can't honestly claim a return.

Most "AI ROI" numbers fall apart the moment you poke them. They're measured on the demo, on cherry-picked examples, or against a "before" nobody actually recorded. Here is the method that stands up — the same one a careful team can reproduce without trusting anyone's word.

  1. Pick one number your business already cares about

    Not five goals — one. Hours spent on a task, cost per case, error rate, time-to-answer. If a stranger on your team wouldn't recognise it as important, it's the wrong number.

  2. Set a clean baseline before you build

    "Before vs after" is meaningless without a real before. Measure how the task performs today, on real work, and write it down. This is the step almost everyone skips — and it's the one that makes the ROI believable.

  3. Measure after on fresh, human-checked data

    Test on work the system hasn't seen, not the examples it was tuned on. Have a person check a slice of the results by hand. A number the vendor computes in private isn't evidence.

  4. Write down the sample size and the caveats

    A win on 400 cases means something; a win on 4 means nothing. Note how big the sample was, who checked it, and what the number does not tell you. Honesty about limits is what makes the number trustworthy.

  5. Turn the number into money or time

    Translate it: minutes saved per case × cases per month × the cost of that time. Now the metric ladders up to something a budget owner feels — the actual return.

The vanity metrics that fake ROI

These look like returns and measure almost nothing useful:

  • Model accuracy on a clean test set. Your real Tuesday inputs are messy; a tidy benchmark score doesn't survive them.
  • Benchmark wins. "Beats model X on task Y" is a press release. Nobody's afternoon got shorter because of it.
  • Usage and engagement. People clicking the AI button is not people getting work done faster. Measure the work, not the tool.
  • A single amazing example. One great output proves it can be right, not that it usually is. Measure over a sample, never an anecdote.

The honesty test

Before you start, write down what result would make you call the project a failure. If you can't name a number that would count as "it didn't work," you're not measuring ROI — you're setting up a story that can only end in success.

Why the method is the point

Anyone can show you a number. What makes a number trustworthy is a method you could run yourself and get the same answer. That's the whole game: a metric your business cares about, a real baseline, a fresh human-checked sample, and the caveats in plain sight. Get those right and the ROI is real. Skip any of them and it's theater with a decimal point.
